Prompting
Writing instructions to an AI to get useful output. A good prompt specifies: Role (who the AI should be), Task (what to do), Format (how to structure output), and Constraints (limits to respect).
🧑‍💼 Role → 📋 Task → 🎯 Format → ⚠️ Constraints
1
Your text becomes numbers. The AI breaks your prompt into chunks called tokens (roughly one word each), converting each into a number. "You are a startup attorney" becomes something like [1342, 517, 23, 8801]. The model only ever sees numbers — never words.
2
It travels via API. Those numbers are sent over the internet through an API — a pipeline to the AI company's servers. Everything in your prompt leaves your device. For client matters, this is why confidentiality clauses around AI tools matter.
3
The model predicts word by word. The AI doesn't "think" — it predicts the most statistically likely next word, then the next. A specific prompt steers those predictions. A vague prompt lets the model wander into generic territory.
4
The response streams back token by token. Words appear one by one as each token is predicted and returned — not because the AI is typing, but because tokens are sent the moment they're generated. The whole process takes under a second.

Iteration
Refining AI output across multiple turns — adjusting your prompt, asking follow-ups, improving the result. Notice how your response quality changed based on how specific your prompt was? That's iteration in action. Professional-quality outputs typically take 3–5 rounds.
Draft → Review → Refine → Repeat ↺
💡 Notice: The quality of the AI's response depends directly on how specific your prompt was. The more context you give, the better the output.
1
The AI has no memory between sessions. Each time you open a new chat, the model starts blank. It has no idea who you are, what you worked on yesterday, or what your client said last week. Everything it knows is only what you've typed in this window — the context window.
2
Your whole conversation is re-sent every turn. When you send a follow-up, the AI receives the entire conversation from the beginning — every single time. This is how it "remembers" what you discussed. Longer conversations mean more tokens processed (and potentially billed) on each turn.
3
Iteration works because context accumulates. When you refine — "make it shorter" or "add a governing law clause" — you're adding steering instructions on top of the full history. The model reprocesses everything and steers its predictions differently. This is why 3–5 rounds typically outperforms trying to write one perfect prompt.
4
Temperature controls creativity vs. precision. Behind the scenes, a setting called temperature (0–1) controls how predictable the AI's word choices are. Legal tools run at low temperature (0.1–0.3) for consistent output. Higher temperature = more creative but more likely to hallucinate.

Hallucination
When an AI produces false information with full confidence — a fabricated case citation, wrong statute, or invented deadline. It sounds authoritative. It isn't. Verify everything: citations, numbers, dates — every time.
⚖️ "The court held in Smith v. Jones (2018)..." → Does this case exist? Always check.
1
The AI is a prediction engine, not a search engine. It doesn't look things up — it predicts the most statistically plausible next word. "Green v. Miller, 42 Cal.App.5th..." sounds like a real citation because the AI has seen thousands of real ones. It generates the pattern, not the fact.
2
It has no "I don't know" signal. The model was trained to always produce a response. It doesn't experience uncertainty — it just keeps predicting. A fabricated citation and a real one look identical to the model at generation time. Both are just the next likely token.
3
RAG is how legal AI tools reduce this risk. Tools like Harvey, Lexis AI, and Westlaw AI first retrieve real documents from a verified database, then feed those into the context window. The model generates from real source text — dramatically reducing hallucinations for citations and statutes.
4
Your professional liability doesn't disappear. Even RAG-powered tools can err. A fabricated citation in a filing is your malpractice exposure, not the AI company's — most terms of service explicitly disclaim liability for legal use. Verification is always your job.

Output Format
Telling the AI exactly how to structure its answer. Lawyers love structure — specify it. "Respond as a numbered memo." "Use plain English." "List risks in order of severity." Without this, the AI guesses — and usually guesses wrong for legal work.
📝 "(1) Summary (2) Key risks (3) Recommended next steps"
1
Structure is inferred, not automatic. "Tell me about HIPAA breach rules" → the model sees a student question → returns a textbook paragraph. "Draft a client action memo" → the model sees a professional task → returns structured output. Same knowledge, completely different shape — based purely on how the prompt was phrased.
2
System prompts set the baseline behaviour. Every AI product (ChatGPT, Claude, Harvey) has a hidden system prompt — instructions written by the company that run before your message. These set the persona, tone, and limits. You never see it, but it shapes every response you get.
3
Fine-tuning bakes in domain expertise. A general model knows a little about everything. A fine-tuned model has been further trained on legal documents, contracts, and case law — better calibrated for legal structure and risk flags. Harvey and Casetext are fine-tuned. ChatGPT is not. This affects both quality and hallucination rates.
4
Your format instructions override everything. Explicit format instructions in your prompt override the system prompt's style and the model's defaults. "Respond only in plain English bullet points under 10 words each" will produce exactly that. Format is the highest-leverage part of any prompt for legal work.
